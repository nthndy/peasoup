I can do it in a 42L bucket, so why not a 3600L chlorine-bleached communal fountain? It can't possibly be that difficult riiiighhttt?

![Communal fountain with chlorine tablet stains]({{ site.baseurl }}/assets/img/fountain_chlorine.jpg)

The basic idea is to take this sterile environment and convert it into a self-cleaning, biologically balanced wildlife pond, replete with a variety of native plants and minibeasts that will hopefully help encourage other wildlife into the already-beautiful communal garden. A few conversations were had, people seemed to be very keen, and before I knew it the fountain was emptied, scrubbed and refilled. 

![The communal fountain, emptied and scrubbed]({{ site.baseurl }}/assets/img/fountain_clean.jpg)

Prior to adding delicate and somewhat expensive aquatic plants, I wanted to assess the quality of the water on a couple of parameters. Given the previous state of the pond, I needed to address the chlorine content first. Chlorine will evaporate off on its own given time, but modern tap water also carries chloramine, which is chlorine bonded to ammonia, and that bond makes it stable enough that it won't just gas off. It needs a dechlorinating solution to shift it. If any were left, nothing would thrive, and it would take out the beneficial bacteria I'm relying on to run the nitrogen cycle down the line.

The less obvious one is Legionella. Warm, still, stagnant water is exactly it's kind of environment, so as an insuring factor I sent a test sample off to a certified laboratory.

![Legionnaires' test]({{ site.baseurl }}/assets/img/legion_test_init.jpg)

While that was away I ran the water through a MARS Fishcare kit for a baseline of the nutrient load.

![Test kit vials against the colour chart]({{ site.baseurl }}/assets/img/testing_init.jpg)

| | reading |
|---|---|
| pH | 8-9 |
| Ammonia | 0 ppm |
| Nitrite | 0 ppm |
| Phosphate | 10 ppm |

The Legionnaires' test came back negative, which was a relief. The chemistry was a mixed bag. Ammonia and nitrite both sat at zero, exactly what you want at this stage: nothing has started decomposing yet, so there's nothing for the nitrogen cycle to work on. Phosphate, though, was pinned to the top of the chart at 10 ppm, and phosphate is what algae thrives on. The pH was high too, up around 8 to 9 where London tap usually runs 7 to 8. Perhaps this was the concrete fountain leaching a bit of lime? Although there is some sort of liner to it. At this point I was ready for the experiment to go full steam ahead.
